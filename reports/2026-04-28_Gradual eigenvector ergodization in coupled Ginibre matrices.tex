{\LARGE \textbf{Gradual eigenvector ergodization in coupled Ginibre matrices}}\\[0.4em]
{\large Margherita Disertori and Yan V. Fyodorov}\\[0.3em]
\href{https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23708}{arXiv:2604.23708}\\[0.3em]
\textbf{Scholar Inbox digest date: 2026-04-28}
\end{center}
\section*{Executive summary}
This paper studies a solvable model for how eigenvectors of a non-Hermitian system gradually lose subsystem identity when two chaotic blocks are coupled. The matrix has block form
\[
X=\begin{pmatrix}G_1 & cI_N \\ cI_N & G_2\end{pmatrix},
\]
where $G_1,G_2$ are independent complex Ginibre matrices and $c\in\mathbb{C}$ controls coupling strength. When $c=0$, each eigenvector lives in exactly one subsystem; when $|c|$ grows, weight spreads across both blocks. The main theorem gives an explicit large-$N$ formula, at $z=0$, for the joint distribution of the subsystem weights.
\section*{Problem setup and intuition}
Writing an eigenvector as $v=(v_1,v_2)^\top$ with $\lVert v\rVert_2=1$, define
\[
p_1=\lVert v_1\rVert_2^2,\qquad p_2=\lVert v_2\rVert_2^2,\qquad p_1+p_2=1.
\]
The limiting density of $(p_1,p_2)$ is given explicitly in terms of modified Bessel functions. This captures the crossover from localization in one block to near-equipartition across both blocks. For fixed $c=O(1)$, the mean eigenvalue density at the origin stays equal to $2/\pi$, so eigenvector ergodization is visible before the bulk spectral density changes.
\section*{Main theorem / result}
For eigenvectors attached to eigenvalues near $z=0$, the normalized joint density converges as $N\to\infty$ to
\[
\pi_\infty(0,p_1,p_2)
=
\frac{|c|^2}{p_1p_2}
\exp\!\left[-|c|^2\!\left(\frac{p_1}{p_2}+\frac{p_2}{p_1}\right)\right]
\delta(p_1+p_2-1)
\Bigg[
I_1(2|c|^2)+\frac12\!\left(\frac{p_1}{p_2}+\frac{p_2}{p_1}\right)I_0(2|c|^2)
\Bigg],
\]
where $I_0$ and $I_1$ are modified Bessel functions. In the weak-coupling limit the mass sits near $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$; for strong fixed coupling the distribution sharpens near $(1/2,1/2)$.
\section*{Proof architecture}
The proof combines an exact finite-$N$ integral representation with a saddle-point/Laplace analysis. The block structure reduces the dependence on the eigenvector to the norm fractions $p_1,p_2$, then the large-$N$ asymptotics localize the radial variables near a simple saddle. The remaining angular integral produces modified Bessel functions, which is why the final answer is fully explicit.
\section*{Why it matters / takeaway}
The main value of the paper is that it turns a qualitative physical story into a sharp random-matrix statement. It gives an analytic benchmark for non-Hermitian many-body chaos and separates eigenvector mixing from spectral-density reorganization. The model is special, but as a theory benchmark it is unusually clean and useful.
