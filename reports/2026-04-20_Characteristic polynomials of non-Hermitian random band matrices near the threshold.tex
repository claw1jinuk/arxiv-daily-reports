\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{Theory Report: Characteristic polynomials of non-Hermitian random band matrices near the threshold}}\\[0.5em]
{\large Mariya Shcherbina}\\[0.25em]
\texttt{arXiv:2604.15355} \quad \url{https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15355}
\end{center}

\textbf{Paper information.} The paper studies the second correlation function of characteristic polynomials for Gaussian non-Hermitian random band matrices in the critical regime $W\asymp \sqrt N$. Earlier work identified delocalized and localized regimes on opposite sides of this scale; this paper analyzes the threshold itself and derives a new limiting object described by a semigroup generated by an explicit differential operator.

\section*{Executive summary}
The observable is the normalized second correlation function of characteristic polynomials,
\[
\Theta(z_1,z_2)=\mathbb E\big[\det(H-z_1)\det(H^*-\bar z_2)\big],
\]
for a non-Hermitian random band matrix with bandwidth $W$. Prior results showed a phase transition: when $W\gg\sqrt N$ the limit matches the Ginibre ensemble, while when $W\ll\sqrt N$ the correlation essentially factorizes. The present paper resolves the crossover point itself.

The main theorem states that if $W=\kappa_*N^{1/2}(1+o(1))$, then the normalized correlation converges to
\[
(e^{\mathbb A_0}\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1),
\]
where $\mathbb A_0$ is an explicit second-order differential operator on $[-1,1]$. So the threshold is neither Ginibre nor factorized; it is governed by a genuinely new critical semigroup. This is exactly the sort of statement one wants at an Anderson-type transition: a nontrivial interpolating object rather than a crude dichotomy.

\section*{Problem setup and intuition}
A random band matrix interpolates between local and mean-field behavior by restricting strong correlations to a window of width $W$ around the diagonal. In Hermitian settings, the scale $W\sim \sqrt N$ is already famous as the expected threshold between localization-like and delocalization-like behavior for certain observables. This paper asks for the analogous threshold phenomenon in a non-Hermitian model.

Instead of studying eigenvalue spacings directly, the paper uses the second correlation of characteristic polynomials. This is a softer but highly informative probe of local spectral structure. In many random-matrix problems, such correlation functions are both analytically tractable and sensitive to phase changes.

The intuition is that transfer-matrix evolution across the band direction should have two competing tendencies. If the band is wide, mixing is strong enough to recover Ginibre-type behavior. If the band is narrow, correlations do not propagate far and the characteristic-polynomial correlation decouples. At the threshold, the top spectral modes of the transfer operator interact nontrivially, producing a critical limiting evolution rather than a degenerate one.

\section*{Main result}
The first background theorem from earlier work says that the normalized correlation tends to the Ginibre limit when $W^2/(N\log^2 N)\to\infty$, but tends to a factorized limit in the opposite wide localized regime. The new theorem addresses the missing case $W\sim\kappa_*\sqrt N$.

Precisely, if $W=\kappa_*N^{1/2}(1+o(1))$, then
\[
\frac{\Theta(z_1,z_2)}{\Theta(z_1,z_1)^{1/2}\Theta(z_2,z_2)^{1/2}}
\longrightarrow (e^{\mathbb A_0}\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1),
\]
with
\[
\mathbb A_0\varphi(z)=\frac{1}{8(\kappa_*u_*)^2}\Big((1-z^2)\varphi''(z)-2z\varphi'(z)\Big)+2z\varphi(z),\qquad z\in[-1,1].
\]
The limit is therefore encoded by a Sturm--Liouville-type generator plus a multiplication term. This is the mathematically crisp signature of crossover criticality.

\section*{Proof architecture}
\textbf{1. SUSY transfer-matrix representation.} The starting point is a supersymmetric integral formula developed in earlier work. It rewrites the characteristic-polynomial correlation as a power of a transfer operator, which is the natural way to expose dependence on the band length.

\textbf{2. Spectral analysis of the transfer kernel.} The operator is decomposed into dominant and subdominant modes. A large part of the proof is showing that the relevant eigenfunctions concentrate in the correct region and that off-diagonal blocks decay fast enough to isolate the effective dynamics.

\textbf{3. Critical rescaling.} At $W\sim\sqrt N$, the spectral gap structure of the transfer operator changes scale. The proof zooms into this window and shows that the discrete transfer dynamics converge to a continuous semigroup generated by $\mathbb A_0$.

\textbf{4. Control of remainders.} The technically heavy middle sections prove that all other transfer-matrix contributions are negligible compared with the critical block. This is what turns the heuristic ``diffusion on modes'' picture into a theorem.

The logic is standard in spirit but hard in execution: identify a transfer operator, isolate its leading critical block, derive its continuum limit, and prove everything else is lower order.

\section*{Why it matters, limitations, and takeaway}
The paper gives a mathematically explicit critical object at the non-Hermitian band threshold. That is valuable because threshold regimes are often the hardest to characterize: away from criticality one usually lands in already-known universality classes, but at criticality one expects a new crossover law.

A limitation is that the analysis is carried out for a Gaussian model and for characteristic-polynomial correlations rather than the full eigenvalue process. So the result is best read as a rigorous first window into the transition, not the final word on non-Hermitian localization-delocalization.

The takeaway is simple: for non-Hermitian random band matrices, $W\sim\sqrt N$ is a true critical regime, and its spectral signature is an explicit nontrivial semigroup rather than either Ginibre universality or complete factorization.
