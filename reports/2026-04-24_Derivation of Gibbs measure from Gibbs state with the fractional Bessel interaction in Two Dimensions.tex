\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{Theory Report: Derivation of Gibbs measure from Gibbs state with the fractional Bessel interaction in Two Dimensions}}\\[0.5em]
{\large Phan Thanh Nam, Rongchan Zhu, and Xiangchan Zhu}\\[0.25em]
\texttt{arXiv:2604.21583} \quad \url{https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21583}
\end{center}

\textbf{Paper information.} The paper derives a classical nonlinear Gibbs measure on $\mathbb T^2$ from a renormalized grand-canonical quantum Bose gas with fractional-Bessel pair interaction $\widehat v_\beta(k)=\langle k\rangle^{-\beta}$ in the full singular range $3/2<\beta\le 2$. The novelty is that the Fourier coefficients are not summable, so the usual density-square renormalization breaks down and a more delicate zero-mode/high-frequency analysis is required.

\section*{Executive summary}
This is a quantum-to-classical limit theorem in a singular regime. The authors consider a two-dimensional bosonic Gibbs state at temperature of order $\lambda^{-1}$ and ask whether, as $\lambda\downarrow0$, it converges to a classical field theory Gibbs measure. For regular enough interactions this is known, but here the Bessel interaction is so singular that the naive density-square rewrite of the quantum interaction generates an infinite self-energy.

The main result still proves convergence. After an adjusted renormalization---keeping nonzero modes in genuine quartic form and centering only the zero mode---the relative free energy converges to the classical partition function, and the $k$-particle reduced density matrices converge in Hilbert--Schmidt norm to the classical $k$-point correlation operators.

Conceptually, the paper shows that the quantum-to-classical program survives beyond the summable-kernel setting, but only after a multiscale decomposition that isolates zero-mode, shell, and tail remainders.

\section*{Problem setup and intuition}
The one-body space is $L^2(\mathbb T^2)$ with free Hamiltonian $h=-\Delta+1$, and the interaction has Fourier coefficients
\[
\widehat v_\beta(k)=\langle k\rangle^{-\beta},\qquad \frac32<\beta\le 2.
\]
The classical target measure is the Gibbs measure
\[
 d\nu(u)=z_{\mathrm{cl}}^{-1}e^{-D(u)}d\mu_0(u),
\]
where $\mu_0$ is the Gaussian free field with covariance $h^{-1}$ and $D(u)$ is the Wick-renormalized nonlinear energy.

The quantum difficulty is immediate. If one rewrites the two-body interaction in terms of density modes $\rho_k$, the commutation relations produce a self-energy proportional to
\[
\sum_k \widehat v_\beta(k)\,\mathcal N,
\]
which diverges because $\widehat v_\beta\notin \ell^1(\mathbb Z^2)$ in the full range considered. So the standard route used for summable kernels fails at the operator level, not merely in an estimate.

The authors' fix is to renormalize only the zero mode via a centered number-fluctuation term and keep the nonzero modes in their original quartic form. The rest of the paper is devoted to proving that this renormalized model still converges to the desired classical Gibbs measure.

\section*{Main theorem}
The paper's main theorem states that as $\lambda\downarrow0$,
\[
-\log \frac{Z_\lambda^{\mathrm{re}}}{Z_0}\to -\log z_{\mathrm{cl}},
\]
and for every fixed $k\ge 1$,
\[
k!\lambda^k\Gamma_\lambda^{(k)}\longrightarrow \int |u^{\otimes k}\rangle\langle u^{\otimes k}|\,d\nu(u)
\qquad\text{in }\mathfrak S^2.
\]
So one gets both free-energy convergence and operator-level convergence of reduced density matrices. The latter is stronger than convergence of scalar moments alone and confirms that the entire low-order correlation structure of the quantum state converges to the classical field theory.

\section*{Proof architecture}
\textbf{1. Renormalized Hamiltonian.} The interaction is rewritten so that only the zero mode is extracted and centered. This yields a well-defined renormalized Gibbs state even though the full density-square decomposition is illegal.

\textbf{2. Variational free-energy framework.} The authors work with the Gibbs variational principle and compare the interacting quantum free energy to finite-dimensional classical Hartree functionals after low-frequency projection.

\textbf{3. First cutoff and high-frequency decomposition.} A low-frequency projector $P$ is introduced. The discrepancy between the full interaction and its projected version is then decomposed into several remainder terms. The paper's structure makes clear that not all remainders can be treated the same way.

\textbf{4. Zero-mode remainder.} A recent second-order correlation inequality is used to control localized fluctuation observables, converting second moments into manageable commutator terms. This yields a quantitative vanishing theorem for the zero-mode high-frequency remainder.

\textbf{5. Shell and tail analysis.} A second cutoff splits the remaining nonzero-mode error into shell and tail pieces. The shell term is handled through mode-by-mode fluctuation bounds and commutator estimates. The tail term uses a different mechanism: positivity of the full Bessel operator and careful off-diagonal control.

\textbf{6. Quantum de Finetti and passage to density matrices.} Once the free-energy lower and upper bounds match, quantitative de Finetti estimates connect the projected quantum state to a classical measure, and the reduced density matrices converge in Hilbert--Schmidt norm.

The proof is a good example of a modern field-theory derivation: variational principles provide the outer structure, while the singularity is managed by multiscale operator inequalities.

\section*{Why it matters, limitations, and takeaway}
The paper pushes the quantum-to-classical Gibbs-measure program into a regime where the fixed interaction is itself ultraviolet-singular. That is mathematically important because it shows the program is not confined to smooth or summable kernels.

The price is complexity. The method is highly tailored to the Bessel interaction and to the two-dimensional torus setting; extending it below $\beta=3/2$ or to other singular kernels would require new ideas, and the paper openly says so.

The takeaway is that the obstruction from non-summability is real but not fatal: with the right renormalization and a finely tuned high-frequency decomposition, the renormalized quantum Bose gas still converges to the expected classical Gibbs field.
